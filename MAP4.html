What is a body?
What is my body in relation to context and references?

Introduction & reflection

I wanted to experiment with form in this workbook. About a year ago, I worked on a project with a friend to create my own website using HTML coding, and that was my very first encounter with a programming language. Coding felt like something completely different from the languages I had known before. It can be very simple and minimal, but if you want to go into detail, you can endlessly build tiny elements. And if something isn’t precise, it simply doesn’t work at all. 😓

I was also intrigued by the fact that coding uses terms like “head” and “body.” In HTML, the body tag is used to define the main content of a web page, including text, images, links, and other elements. It is a container for essential content, and the idea of “containing” something made the term “body” particularly fascinating to me. In that sense, I began to think that not only in coding but also in my own life, the body functions similarly. Just as genes evolve through generations, carrying trauma and information, my body is constantly accumulating and releasing emotions and memories.

Through this, I believe I can engage with the MAP4 question: What is (my) body in relation to contexts and references? This is also a challenge for me, as someone who has felt distant from technology. Even with my limited knowledge of coding, I want to use this project as an opportunity to reach into a new field. I hope that form and content will come together in a way that creates synergy.

I also included in this workbook the “Langsam Reading Klein Writing Club” project, where we read and write together. I’ve always had a desire to write, but whenever I tried to begin, it felt overwhelming. So together with my friend and fellow artist, we created a writing project that is not grand or intimidating, but small, consistent, and accessible. The basic structure is that once a week, we each bring a poem we want to share, read and discuss it together, and then use the conversation as a prompt to write our own poems.

Even with a poem of around ten lines, there is an endless flow of references, experiences, and stories to share. It was fascinating to see how each person resonates with different parts of the same poem, how a single word can open up countless directions of thought. In this way, poetry connects again with the idea of the body as something that holds and contains. It also led me to think about the performativity of poetry—a poem itself can become a score for movement, and beyond simply reading it, I began to wonder how it can be voiced through me as a medium. I was reminded of something a director I once worked with said: “Theatre is poetry written with the body.”
This understanding deepened when I encountered the idea that the word “poetry” originates from the Greek poiesis, from poiein, meaning “to do.” In this sense, poetry is not merely something written or read, but something enacted—it exists through doing, through the body. In contrast to “the Word” as something abstract and immaterial, poetry insists on presence and embodiment. I realized that when a poem is performed, the act of uttering it transforms it entirely—it is no longer just language, but an event that moves through the body and into space. This realization affirmed my interest in translating poetry into physical expression, where the body becomes both a medium and a site of meaning, carrying, shaping, and activating the poem beyond text.

Moving further, I would like to explore writing in my mother language as well as in languages I am not native to. Each language has its own ways of expression, and when there are limitations in unfamiliar languages, I am curious about how writing can still take place. I want to begin from these questions and explore new possibilities. Although this project is still in its early stages, I hope to expand it by hosting sessions with more people. I am also interested in exploring how the poems created through this project could be compiled into a published collection, or developed into other formats such as performances or music.💫

A Small Room
I went to you
because I wanted to make a home within a small room,
and live inside it.
With nothing but tender red shoots to my name,
I turned my pockets inside out
and plcaed a handful of soil in your hands.

In that soil, there were unhatched ant eggs,
torn fragments of a cicada’s wing,
and even a drop of the pus you had shed.
In the small room,
tiny dots were grotesquely embedded everywhere.
With chopsticks, slowly, one by one,
I moved those little dots into the soil.
Then I went into that handful of earth,
relieved myself,
and felt the warmth spreading gently.
It was too damp to build a home.

So we lay our heads on ant eggs,
covered ourselves with cicada wings,
and took a painfully warm nap.
When we woke,
the handful of soil burst, pop!
We swept the floor with our hair
and wiped the windows with nosebleed.
We had to start all over again.
From the beginning.
From the beginning.
From the beginning.

But we know in a small room,
that’s only natural.
So again,
I wanted to go to you
to make a home within that small room
and live inside it.
