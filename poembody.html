Focus in Real Time

By June Jordan

Poem for Margaret who passed the California bar!

A bowl of rice 

      as food
      as politics
      or metaphor
      as something valuable and good
      or something common to consume/ exploit/ ignore
      
Who grew these grains
Who owned the land
Who harvested these soft particles to money
Who kept the cash
Who shipped the consequences of the cash
Who else was going to eat the rice
Who else was going to convert the rice to cash
        
Who would design the flowers for the outside of the bowl
Who would hold the bowl between her hands
Who would give the bowl away
Who could share the rice
Who could fill that bowl with rice how many times a day
                how many times a week
Who would adore the hands that held the bowl that held the rice
Who would adore the look the smell the stream of boiled rice
                    in a bowl
        
Who will analyze the cash the rice becomes
Who will sit ceside the bowl or fight for rice
Who will write about the hands that hold the bowl
Who will want to own land
              A bowl of rice

Body

By Kae Tempest 

The way she saw it, it was hers to hate.
Baggy jeans, shirts that came down to her knees
Or tops so short she couldn't stand up straight.


Grown-up voices crossed the street, girl you'll freeze.
Pale neighbours, their own lives being routine,
Swivelled when she passed, rattling their keys.


She liked it when it hurt. Liked playing mean.
She liked being where no one else would go.
She liked to not remember where she'd been.


at home later, she watched the water flow
And answered all their questions withouth words.
Some things are better picked before they grow.


She grabs fistful of belly fat. it hurts.
She wishes she could cut away her hips.
Instead, she goes to work, salutes the birds.


Pissing it down, her las cigarette rips.
Hot chocolate in a big cup, tiny sips.

Tongueless Mother Tongue

By Kim Hyesoon 

Follwing the publication of my poetry collection I'm OK, I'm Pig!, 
I received a call from a local organization about an award the book had won. 
After the announcement, soem people argued that I should not be given the award because 
I had disparaged the people of the local community as 'pigs' or because I was a modernist. 
That's when I found out for the first time that I was a modernist. Some people even threatened 
to use violence if I should show up to receive the award. With the help of a friend clued up 
on social media, I read all those comments, and immediately declinced the award. 
I'm OK, I'm Pig! may seem to deal primarily with pigs slaughtered in an outbreak of 
foot-and-mouth disease. However, taking those pigs as a starting point, I developed poetry as
'the body' of all living things including humans. At the same time, I reflected on how our 
bodies—including the bodies of other humans—had been arrested by our gaze and treated with violence.
I was staying at t Buddhist temple in Namhae, and every time the monk's words fell upon us
sitting in the lotus position, my body felt like that of a pig inside a Zen room. My body had never
felt more abject. I floated up into the air, and looked down at my body. The body of a pig eating a
pig. I found myself muttering poems in opposition to, or as an excuse for the monk's words of
reproach towards our bodies indulging in sensual appetites. Having a body means having 'senses' and
feeling pain. As a pig on a silent swine planet watching from afar, I saw myself on earth decapitating
pigs, ridiculing them, and then finally eating them. Pigs with the same senses as me—a human with
a view point—existed as embodied begins in the same bodies even on a different planet.

'To keep the pain from translating itself into memory'
